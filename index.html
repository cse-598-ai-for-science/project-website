Abstract

Predicting the value of stock options remains a critical challenge in financial markets. Traditional valuation models, such as those based on the Black-Scholes equation, rely solely on classical financial data. This project proposes a novel framework that integrates both traditional financial metrics and alternative data sources (such as social media sentiment and economic policy uncertainty) to predict option values. We implement a knowledge-transfer mechanism where a Black-Scholes-informed source model (PINN) guides data-driven target models. By embedding domain knowledge within a data-driven architecture, our approach aims to enhance predictive accuracy and enable investors to capture incremental informational advantages at a lower cost.

Methodology

Our approach sits at the confluence of classical financial option-pricing models and knowledge-guided machine learning. We utilized a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) as a source model to learn the latent dynamics of the Black-Scholes world.

This theoretical knowledge is transferred to target models (using Gated LSTM architectures) that process both market data and alternative signals. This allows the model to respect financial no-arbitrage constraints while learning from messy, real-world data like news sentiment.

Key Results & Conclusions

  • Hybrid Superiority: Models combining physics constraints with data-driven approaches consistently outperformed pure data-driven baselines.
  • Architecture Matters: The Gated LSTM architecture proved superior to standard MLPs, reducing RMSE significantly across configurations.
  • Value of Alternative Data: Incorporating news sentiment provided a tangible information edge. However, combining too many noisy sources (e.g., Sentiment + EPU) led to diminishing returns, highlighting the need for careful feature selection.

The table above quantifies the "grey box" advantage. While strict physics enforcement in data-rich environments can sometimes degrade fit, the hybrid model with targeted sentiment data achieved the global minimum RMSE. This proves that theoretical priors act best as a guide rather than a hard constraint when combined with high-signal alternative data.
